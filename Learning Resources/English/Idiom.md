Business English Idioms


Idiom 1:
To Call it a Day: (Meaning to stop working on something)
Example: 
- After finishing the report, Sarah decided to call it a day and went home early.
- After hours of studying, John felt it was time to call it a day and relax.


Idiom 2: 
To Get your Foot in the Door: (Meaning to take a first step towards a goal, especially in business)
Example:
- Landing an internship at a prestigious company helped Mark get his foot in the door for future job opportunities.
- Volunteering at the event was a great way for Lisa to get her foot in the door of the marketing industry.

Idiom 3:
To see Eye to Eye: (Meaning to agree with someone)
Example:
- After a long discussion, the team finally saw eye to eye on the project direction.
- The two business partners saw eye to eye on the company's expansion plans.

Idiom 4:
To pay under the Table: (Meaning to pay someone secretly, often to avoid taxes or regulations)
Example:
- The contractor offered to do the work for cash paid under the table to avoid paperwork.
- Some small businesses pay their employees under the table to reduce costs.

Idiom 5:
To Talk Shop: (Meaning to discuss work-related topics informally)
Example:
- During the lunch break, the engineers couldn't help but talk shop about the new technology.
- Even at social gatherings, the sales team often talks shop about their latest deals and strategies.
